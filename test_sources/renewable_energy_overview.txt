Renewable Energy in Sub-Saharan Africa: An Overview

Author: International Energy Research Institute
Year: 2023

Sub-Saharan Africa holds some of the world's largest untapped renewable energy
resources. Solar irradiance across the Sahel averages 5-7 kWh/m2/day, among
the highest globally. Wind resources along the East African Rift Valley and
southern coastal zones are substantial, with mean speeds exceeding 7 m/s at
hub height in the best sites.

Current electricity access rates remain low. As of 2022, approximately 600
million people in sub-Saharan Africa lacked access to electricity, representing
roughly 43% of the global total of people without power. Rural electrification
rates in countries such as Chad, Niger, and South Sudan remain below 5%.

Solar photovoltaic (PV) costs have fallen dramatically over the past decade.
Utility-scale solar PV levelised cost of electricity (LCOE) dropped from
approximately $0.36/kWh in 2010 to under $0.05/kWh in 2022 for best-in-class
projects. This cost reduction has made solar the cheapest source of new
electricity generation in many African markets.

Mini-grid systems powered by solar-battery or solar-diesel hybrid configurations
are increasingly deployed in rural areas where grid extension is uneconomical.
More than 3,000 mini-grids are operational across Africa, with concentrated
deployment in Nigeria, Tanzania, Ethiopia, and Madagascar.

Key barriers to renewable energy deployment include: limited access to
long-term concessional finance; weak grid infrastructure; lack of bankable
project pipelines; foreign exchange risk; and policy uncertainty.

The African Development Bank's New Deal on Energy for Africa aims to achieve
universal access by 2025 through a combination of grid extension, mini-grids,
and stand-alone solar systems. Total investment required is estimated at
$70 billion per year through 2030.
