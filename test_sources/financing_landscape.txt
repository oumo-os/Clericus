Financing the Energy Transition in Africa

Author: Climate Finance Analytics
Year: 2023

Total clean energy investment in Africa reached $30 billion in 2022, a record
high but still far below the $90-100 billion annual requirement estimated to
achieve universal energy access and meet Nationally Determined Contribution
(NDC) targets by 2030.

SOURCES OF CAPITAL

Public Development Finance: Multilateral development banks and bilateral
donors accounted for approximately 40% of total clean energy investment in
2022. The African Development Bank committed $25 billion to energy access
over 2019-2024, with a strong focus on renewable generation and transmission.

Private Capital: Private investment reached $12 billion in 2022, concentrated
in South Africa (REIPPPP rounds), Morocco (Noor solar complex), and Kenya
(geothermal and wind). Private participation outside these three markets
remains limited.

Blended Finance: Structures combining concessional public funds with private
capital at risk have shown promise. The Green Climate Fund and Climate
Investment Funds have deployed over $2 billion in blended finance vehicles
specifically targeting least-developed country energy transitions.

THE INVESTMENT GAP

The $60-70 billion annual gap between current investment and requirement is
attributable to several factors: perceived political and macroeconomic risk
exceeding actual default rates; lack of local capital markets depth; insufficient
bankable projects in the pipeline; and short tenor mismatch between 15-25 year
energy project lifetimes and available local currency financing.

EMERGING MECHANISMS

Green bonds denominated in local currency are nascent but growing. Nigeria
issued a $30 million sovereign green bond in 2017, the first in Africa, and
Kenya followed in 2019. Diaspora bonds targeting African communities abroad
have raised financing for infrastructure in Ethiopia and Kenya.

Currency risk hedging facilities — including the Currency Exchange Fund (TCX)
and USAID's Development Credit Authority — provide partial mitigation but
coverage remains insufficient relative to demand.
