Policy Incentives for Renewable Energy Investment

Author: Global Policy Research Center
Year: 2022

Governments across Sub-Saharan Africa have deployed a range of policy
instruments to stimulate renewable energy investment. These fall into four
broad categories: fiscal incentives, regulatory frameworks, direct finance
mechanisms, and market design reforms.

FISCAL INCENTIVES

Import duty exemptions for solar panels, inverters, and batteries have been
adopted in Kenya, Uganda, Rwanda, Tanzania, and Ghana. Kenya's exemption,
introduced in 2014, is credited with accelerating the uptake of off-grid
solar home systems to over 2.5 million units by 2021.

Corporate tax holidays of five to ten years are offered to qualifying
renewable energy projects in Ethiopia, Senegal, and Ivory Coast. Value-added
tax (VAT) exemptions on renewable energy equipment are common across
the region, reducing upfront capital costs by 15-20%.

REGULATORY FRAMEWORKS

Feed-in tariff (FiT) programs guarantee a fixed price for electricity
fed into the national grid over a contract period, typically 15-25 years.
South Africa's Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer Procurement
Programme (REIPPPP) has contracted over 6,400 MW of renewable capacity
since its launch in 2011, making it the continent's most successful
competitive tender programme.

Net metering regulations, adopted in South Africa, Kenya, and Namibia,
allow residential and commercial users with rooftop solar to offset their
electricity bills by feeding surplus power back to the grid.

DIRECT FINANCE MECHANISMS

Development finance institutions (DFIs) — including the International
Finance Corporation, African Development Bank, and bilateral agencies —
provide concessional loans, guarantees, and equity for renewable projects.
Risk mitigation instruments such as the Multilateral Investment Guarantee
Agency (MIGA) and Scaling Solar have been pivotal in reducing perceived
investment risk in frontier markets.

CHALLENGES AND GAPS

Despite these mechanisms, several structural gaps persist. Grid connection
delays average 18-36 months in many countries. Currency risk remains
unmitigated in most markets. Small project size increases transaction costs
relative to deal value. Standardised power purchase agreement (PPA) templates
are absent in most jurisdictions outside South Africa.
